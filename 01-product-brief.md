# 01 — FOODX Product Brief

> The north star document. When in doubt about any product decision, come back here.

---

## Product Identity

**Name:** FOODX  
**Category:** Social Food Delivery Platform  
**Tagline:** *Order together. Eat together.*  
**One-liner:** FOODX is the food delivery app that knows your friends — where ordering food is a shared moment, not a silent transaction.  
**Elevator pitch:** Imagine if food delivery and Instagram had a baby, raised in Ibadan. FOODX lets you order from your favourite local spots, track your rider live, and share the whole experience with your people — all in one app.

---

## Vision

To become the go-to food app for young Africans — not just a delivery tool, but the social platform built around food culture, community, and connection across the continent.

---

## Mission

Make food ordering a shared social moment, not just a transaction. Every order on FOODX should feel like an invitation.

---

## Target Audience

### Primary: Gen Z Students in Ibadan (18–24)

**Where they live:** University of Ibadan, Polytechnic Ibadan, Lead City University hostels, off-campus apartments in Bodija, Agbowo, Sango, and Apata.

**Who they are:**  
They grew up with smartphones. They have never known a world without Instagram. They screenshot menus from restaurants on WhatsApp, argue about where to order from in group chats, and post their plates before they eat. Food is culture, food is social, food is content.

They are broke-ish but strategic. They know what things should cost. They will not pay a ₦1,500 delivery fee if the food costs ₦2,000. But they will happily pay ₦500 if the experience feels right. They respond to value, speed, and anything that feels made for them rather than adapted for them from a Western market.

They are loyal to personality. A brand with a voice, a brand that makes them laugh, a brand that feels Nigerian — that earns repeat usage. A brand that feels corporate gets one order and gets deleted.

**Their relationship with food:** Food is communal. They order together in groups. They compare orders. They call each other to say "I just ordered from that place, their jollof is fire." They use food as a love language — "Let me buy you food" is a genuine gesture of affection. They document food heavily on social media.

**Their relationship with delivery apps:** They have used alternatives (Bolt Food, Chowdeck, local WhatsApp-based options). Their complaints: unreliable riders, cold food, apps that feel foreign, no sense of community, bad customer service. They want an app that is fast, transparent about delivery time, and has a personality.

**Their spending habits:** Cash is available but mobile money and cards are increasingly common. They use Paystack-powered apps daily. They will spend ₦2,000–₦8,000 per order on average, more for group orders.

### Secondary: Young Professionals in Ibadan (24–30)

**Where they work:** Tech hubs (CcHub Ibadan, Rise Hub), corporate offices, and remote workers in Bodija and Ring Road areas.

**Who they are:** Slightly more disposable income than students. They order for lunch at the office. They send food to partners. They appreciate efficiency. They still want the social features but use them differently — less "look at my food" and more "I just sent you lunch."

### Restaurant Partners

Local restaurants ranging from structured chains (Chicken Republic) to beloved informal spots (Item 7, small chops vendors, pepper soup joints). They want: easy onboarding, reliable order flow, quick payouts, and a customer base they wouldn't otherwise reach.

## Tone & Voice

FOODX speaks like a cool older sibling — not trying too hard, always in the know, warm without being mushy. Five principles:

### 1. Nigerian, Not Translated
FOODX was not built in San Francisco and localised for Nigeria. It was built here. Language should reflect that. Not pidgin (that's a different brand), but culturally resonant Nigerian English. "Your rider is almost there" feels global. "Your rider is almost there, hold on!" feels like a real person.

### 2. Warm and Social
Every interaction is an opportunity to feel human. Empty states are not failures — they are invitations. "No orders yet? Your first one's always the most memorable." Not: "You have no orders."

### 3. Playful but Not Cringe
FOODX can joke. It can use food puns. It can be fun. But it never tries too hard. If a joke lands wrong, it damages trust. When in doubt, be warm instead of witty.

### 4. Direct and Honest
When things go wrong, say so plainly. No corporate passive voice. "Your payment didn't go through — check your card details and try again." Not: "There was an issue processing your transaction."

### 5. Food-Obsessed
FOODX genuinely cares about food. Copy should reflect that energy. The app is written by people who get excited about food, not people who manage a logistics operation.

## Feature Philosophy

Every feature must earn its place by answering: **Does this make ordering food more social, or does it make food delivery more reliable?** If it does neither, it does not belong in Phase 1.

**Why social features alongside delivery?** Because ordering food is already social behaviour. People discuss it before, during, and after. FOODX just formalises that and makes it part of the product.

**Why OTP-only auth (no email)?** Because the target user's digital identity is their phone number. Email is an afterthought. The fastest path to a new account is "enter your number, get a code." Full stop.

**Why Paystack?** Because it is the most trusted payment infrastructure in Nigeria. Users already have it embedded in apps they use. Trust transfers.

**Why streaks?** Because consistency is the core driver of delivery revenue. If a user orders 5 days in a row, the streak makes day 6 feel necessary. Gamification is not gimmick — it is retention.

**Why food gifting?** Because "let me buy you food" is a real social behaviour in Nigerian culture. Building it into the product turns an emotional gesture into a product feature. It also drives order volume — one person's decision becomes two people's experience.

---

## What FOODX Is NOT

- **Not a generic delivery app.** FOODX is not competing on the ground of fastest delivery times or widest restaurant selection (yet). It is competing on experience, personality, and social connection.
- **Not a social media app that happens to have food.** The delivery product is real, operational, and must work perfectly. The social features enhance it; they do not replace it.
- **Not built for everyone.** FOODX is for Gen Z in Ibadan first. It is emphatically not trying to serve 45-year-old professionals in Victoria Island in Phase 1.
- **Not a WhatsApp replacement.** In-app chat is for food-related conversations and friend interactions around orders. It is not a general messaging platform.
- **Not a restaurant-first platform.** FOODX is user-first. Restaurants are valued partners, but the product is designed around the customer's experience, not the restaurant's convenience.
- **Not feature-bloated.** Every feature in this doc was deliberately chosen. Scope creep is the enemy. Build what is here, build it well, then expand.
